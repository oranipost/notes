##  Chapter 21: Unemployment

### How Economists Define and Compute Unemployment Rate
*   The adult population is divided into three categories: employed, unemployed, and out of the labor force.
    *   Employed: currently working for pay.
    *   Unemployed: out of work, available to work, and actively looking for a job.
    *   Out of the labor force (not in the labor force): out of paid work and not 
        actively looking for a job.
*   The labor force is the number of employed plus the unemployed.
*   Unemployment rate = (Unemployed / Total Labor Force) * 100.
*   Labor force participation rate = (Total Labor Force / Total Adult Population) *
    100.
*   Hidden unemployment includes underemployed people and discouraged workers.
    *   Such as, an individual with a college degree in finance who is working as a 
        sales clerk is underemployed.
    *   Discouraged workers have stopped looking for employment.
### What Causes Changes in Unemployment over the Short Run
*   Cyclical unemployment is the variation in unemployment caused by the business 
    cycle (moving from expansion to recession).
*   Wages may be "sticky downward," meaning they adjust slowly and rarely decrease.
*   Reasons for sticky wages include:
    *   Implicit contract: employers try to keep wages from falling in bad times, and 
        employees don't expect huge increases in good times.
    *   Efficiency wage theory: workers' productivity depends on their pay.
    *   Adverse selection of wage cuts: if wages are reduced across the board, the  
        best workers will leave.
    *   Insider-outsider model: cutting wages alienates "insiders" who keep the firm 
        running smoothly.
### What Causes Changes in Unemployment over the Long Run
*   The natural rate of unemployment is the remaining level of unemployment that 
    occurs even when the economy is healthy.
*   Frictional unemployment occurs as workers move between jobs.
    *   It takes time to match job seekers with correct job openings.
*   Structural unemployment occurs because individuals lack skills valued by the labor 
    market.
    *   Such as, aerospace engineers unemployed after the space program downsized.
##  Chapter 22: Inflation

### Tracking Inflation

*   Inflation is a general and ongoing rise in the level of prices in an entire 
    economy.
*   Hyperinflation is an extreme form of inflation, such as in Zimbabwe where prices 
    essentially doubled every day.
*   To calculate the price level, economists use a basket of goods and services, 
    consisting of items typically bought by individuals or businesses.
*   Economists typically report the price level as an index number, choosing one base 
    year with an index number equal to 100.

### How to Measure Changes in the Cost of Living

*   Consumer Price Index (CPI) is the most commonly cited measure of inflation in the 
    US, based on a fixed basket of goods for an average family.
*   Substitution bias: assuming consumers buy a fixed basket overstates the true cost 
    of living because people will substitute away from goods whose relative prices 
    have risen.
*   Quality/new goods bias: leaving out quality improvements or new goods overstates   
    inflation.
*   Core inflation index takes the CPI and excludes volatile economic variables, like  
    energy and food prices, to capture underlying trends.
*   Other indices include the Producer Price Index (PPI), GDP deflator, and Employment 
    Cost Index.
### The Confusion Over Inflation
*   Inflation can cause unintended redistributions of purchasing power.
    *   Such as, people holding cash or having fixed-rate pensions lose buying power.
    *   Borrowers paying a fixed interest rate can benefit because they repay loans in 
        less valuable dollars.
*   Inflation blurs price signals, making it difficult for businesses and individuals 
    to react to economic signals.
*   Indexing is when a price, wage, or interest rate is adjusted automatically with 
    inflation.
    *   Such as, cost-of-living adjustments (COLAs) in wage contracts, or 
        adjustable-rate mortgages (ARMs).
##  Chapter 23: The International Trade and Capital Flows

### Measuring Trade Balances
*   Balance of trade (trade balance): The gap between a nation's exports and imports.
*   Merchandise trade balance: Measures only the physical goods transported between 
    countries.
*   Current account balance: A broader measure that includes four components:
    1. Trade in goods.
    2. Trade in services.
    3. Income receipts and payments (money received by domestic investors on foreign 
    investments and vice versa).
    4. Unilateral transfers (payments sent abroad without receiving a direct good or 
    service, such as foreign aid or remittances).

*   Trade Balances and Flows of Financial Capital

    * Trade balances are intimately connected to international flows of financial 
        capital.
        *   A trade deficit means a net inflow of financial capital from abroad 
            (borrowing).
        *   A trade surplus means a net outflow of financial capital to other 
            countries (lending).
*   The National Saving and Investment Identity
    *   Supply of financial capital must equal Demand for financial capital.
    *   Formula: $S + (M - X) = I + (G - T)$.
        *   S: Private savings.
        *   M - X$: Trade deficit (foreign capital inflow).
        *   I: Private sector investment.
        *   G - T$: Government borrowing (budget deficit).
*   A nation's own levels of domestic saving and investment determine its balance of 
    trade.

*   The Difference between Level of Trade and the Trade Balance
    *   Level of trade: Measured by exports as a percentage of GDP, indicating the 
        degree of globalization.
    *   Factors influencing the level of trade:
        *   Size of the economy.
        *   Geographic location (neighbors).
        *   History of trade.
*   A country can have a low level of trade but a high trade deficit (like the U.S.).

##  Chapter 24: The Aggregate Demand/Aggregate Supply Model

### Macroeconomic Perspectives on Demand and Supply

*   Say's Law: "Supply creates its own demand".
    *   Primarily applies in the long run.
    *   Neoclassical economists emphasize this supply-focused view.
*   Keynes' Law: "Demand creates its own supply".
    *   Primarily applies in the short run.
    *   Argues the economy can produce less than potential because a lack of demand 
        leads to inadequate incentives for firms to produce.
*   Building a Model of Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply
*   Aggregate Supply (AS): The total quantity of output (real GDP) firms will produce and sell at each price level.
    *   Slopes upward because higher output prices (with fixed input prices) encourage 
        more production to earn higher profits.
    *   Potential GDP: The maximum amount an economy can produce by fully employing 
        existing resources.
        *   It is shown as a vertical long-run aggregate supply (LRAS) curve.
*   Aggregate Demand (AD): The amount of total spending on domestic goods and services 
    (C + I + G + X - M).
    *   Slopes downward due to three effects:
        1. Wealth effect: Higher prices reduce real wealth, reducing consumption.
        2. Interest rate effect: Higher prices increase the demand for money, driving 
        up interest rates and reducing investment.
        3. Foreign price effect: Higher domestic prices make exports relatively more 
        expensive and imports cheaper, reducing net exports.
*   Equilibrium occurs at the intersection of AS and AD, determining real GDP and the 
    price level.

*   Shifts in Aggregate Supply and Aggregate Demand
    *   AS shifts:
        *   Productivity growth shifts the AS curve to the right in the long run.
        *   Higher prices for key inputs (like energy or labor) shift the short-run AS 
            curve to the left, causing stagflation (stagnant growth and high inflation).
    *   AD shifts:
        *   Higher consumer or business confidence shifts AD to the right.
        *   Increases in government spending or tax cuts shift AD to the right.

*   Keynes' Law and Say's Law in the AD/AS Model
    *   The Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS) curve can be divided into three zones:
        1. Keynesian zone: The flat portion on the far left where AD largely 
        determines output and cyclical unemployment is high.
        2. Neoclassical zone: The steep portion on the right where potential GDP 
        largely determines output and shifts in AD mostly affect price levels.
        3. Intermediate zone: The middle upward-sloping portion where shifts in AD 
        affect both output and price levels.

*   Bank Regulation:

    1.  Reserve Requirements: Banks must keep a % of deposits. They cannot lend 
        everything.
    2.  Capital Requirements: Banks must have enough net worth to cover potential loan 
        losses.
    3.  Deposit Insurance: If a bank fails, the government (FDIC) protects the savers'

    4.  Lender of Last Resort: The Fed lends money to banks during a crisis to stop a

*   How Monetary Policy Works

    1.  Open Market Operations: The Fed buys bonds to lower interest rates and sells 
        bonds to raise them.
    2.  Discount Rate: Higher rates make bank borrowing expensive, which reduces the 
        money supply.
    3.  Reserve Ratio: Forcing banks to hold more cash leaves less money for them to 
        lend out.
    4.  Quantitative Easing (QE): A way to inject money directly when interest rates 
        are already at zero.

*   Pitfalls of Monetary Policy

    1.  Time Lags: It takes months or years for policy changes to actually fix the 
        economy.
    2.  Excess Reserves: If banks are scared, they won't lend money even if the Fed 
        asks them to.
    3.  Unpredictable Velocity: If people stop spending and hoard cash, printing more 
        money won't help.
    4.  Zero Bound: Once interest rates hit 0%, traditional tools stop working.

##  Chapter 29: Exchange Rates and International Capital Flows

### How the Foreign Exchange Market Works
*   Foreign exchange market: The market in which people or firms use one currency to 
    purchase another.
*   Types of international investments:
    *   Foreign direct investment (FDI): Purchasing a firm (at least 10%) in another 
        country; involves managerial responsibility and a long-run focus.
    *   Portfolio investment: A purely financial investment without management 
        responsibility; often short-term.
*   Hedging: Using a financial transaction to protect oneself against currency risk.
*   Appreciating (strengthening): When a currency exchanges for more of other 
    currencies.
*   Depreciating (weakening): When a currency trades for less of other currencies.
    *   A stronger currency benefits domestic tourists abroad and foreign importers, 
        but hurts domestic exporters and foreign tourists visiting.
*   Demand and Supply Shifts in Foreign Exchange Markets
*   Expectations about future exchange rates: Can shift demand and supply, often 
    making currency movements self-reinforcing.
*   Differences in rates of return: Higher interest rates attract funds from abroad, 
    shifting demand to the right and causing the currency to appreciate.
*   Relative inflation: Higher inflation erodes buying power, decreasing demand for 
    the currency and causing it to depreciate.
*   Purchasing Power Parity (PPP): The exchange rate that equalizes the prices of 
    internationally traded goods across countries due to arbitrage.

*   Exchange Rate Policies
    *   Four main exchange rate policies:
        1. Floating exchange rate: The foreign exchange market completely determines 
        the exchange rate.
        2. Soft peg: The market usually determines the rate, but the central bank 
        sometimes intervenes to prevent large fluctuations.
        3. Hard peg: The central bank sets a fixed and unchanging value for the 
        exchange rate.
        4. Merged currency: Eliminating exchange rate risk by sharing a currency (e.
        g., the euro or dollarization), which requires giving up independent monetary 
        policy.
